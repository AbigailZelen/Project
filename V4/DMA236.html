Writing Assignments:
Presentation Markets:

Consumer Publications (magazines): When it comes to consumer publications, you need to do your homework and be well prepared. You should read a few (or many) of the magazine issues to make sure your photographs will be appropriate for the magazine you’re researching. After you’ve done your research, you need to write a letter to the publication. You’ll write a detailed cover letter that will explain why your photographs are appropriate for the magazine and send some of your work. However, if you have a photo essay you wish to submit, you’ll send in a query letter. A query letter is around 1 to 1 ½ pages that explains your qualifications for the photo essay, and you’ll send some jpegs of your photographs. Make sure you find out how the magazine prefers to be contacted. If you’re written cover letter or query letter goes well, you may land an in-person interview.
Summary: Do your homework, if you’re a good fit, send in a query letter and see if you end up with an in-person interview.

Newspapers: Something to keep in mind with newspapers are the strict deadlines. If you miss a date, that’s it. There are no do overs, and newspapers aren’t going to publish old news even if your photograph is a one in a million. When applying to a newspaper, keep in mind that different papers have different qualifications. Some newspapers still only print in black and white. Newspapers mainly rely on their staff to provide photographs and stories, so you need to have a solid photograph/s to submit. If you have a really strong photograph, you will need to negotiate with the newspaper to be payed appropriately as newspapers don’t pay much. However, major news event images often sell many times, so you want to make sure you’re being payed well for your work.
Summary: Do you homework, THERE ARE DEADLINES, have a strong photograph, and keep in mind that you may have to negotiate prices. The book didn’t seem to go over how to apply, but I would check out their website if they have one.

Trade Publications: Trade publications are taken very seriously by both the readers and the editors. To submit photographs to trade publications, you need to demonstrate knowledge with both your photography and techniques, but also demonstrate immense knowledge about the subject you’re photographing. Most of the photographs from Trade publications come from stock images, but if you have skills in the particular area of the trade publication, then you can create a query and get ahold of someone to see about submitting your images. Most of these publications buy 24 or less images a year for their issues.
Summary: Have serious knowledge on the subject, have a stellar image, and keep in mind your image needs to be amazing because trade publications don’t use many photographs (typically). To apply, write a query and contact the trade publication to ask about submission.

Book Publishers: Since publishing markets are so competitive, freelancers need to be flexible. Photographs for book publishers range from text illustrations, promotional material, images on their websites, and of course book covers. Book publishers will either buy photographs from stock images a particular photographer might have or assign a photographer a certain task or thing to photograph. You can submit photographs to a book publisher by sending a cover letter with some of your photographs (with list prices) and link a website if you have one. If you’re putting together a photo book, you may need to create a “dummy” book that will look almost identical to the book you’ll be publishing and send it in to a publisher. As the book says, “It is not always necessary to be well-known to publish your photographs as a book. What you do need, however, is a unique perspective, a salable idea, and quality work.” Larger publishers may pay more up front, however, smaller publishers typically risk more and it might be easier to get your work published.
Summary: There a many different categories for publishing your photographs when it comes to books. So, do your research and be prepared to write a cover letter, put together price lists or “dummy” books, and understand the differences between small and large publishing companies.

Greeting Cards, Posters & Related Products: Make sure you do your research before you attempt to have an industry turn your photographs into a greeting card, poster, etc. After research, you can send in a query to any of the companies you’re interested in and that’d be a good fit for your work. These kinds of companies receive so many submissions, that when they receive a query from you that states what’s available vs some of your photographs. This way, the company will contact you acquiring to what you have available instead of being overwhelmed with photographs. You will also stand out by being different this way. Note: some companies require you to submit photographs, and some require a list and no photographs. Be careful when you sell these photographs to these kinds of companies. They will often pay higher rates, but typically along with higher rates, they buy all the rights to your images. So, you may want to negotiate with the company to only hold the copy right for a specific time. Some of these companies work by paying royalties.
Summary: Know how to stand out because it’s very common for photographers to apply to these companies. Do your research. Understand copy rights and how the company treats copy rights.

Stock Photo Agencies: Stock agencies typically pay you 50 percent of the image price every time they sell your image. These kinds of companies are very competitive.
List of things that you should know/have before contacting a stock agency:

  • Have plenty of images. You need to have way more than you send the agency because if they’re interested, they may want to see more of your work.
  • Be ready to send in new images regularly. If you don’t have an enormous photo library or you don’t take photographs all the time, stock agencies might not be for you.
  • Organize your work. Make sure your files are marked properly. Mark files in a way that they know what’s contained in each image.
  • Research, research, research, and research. Make sure you know about the company you’re going to work for, how they work, how big the agency is, etc.
  • Check and see if how the company treats their employees, and how professional they are (or not).
  • Once you’ve decided to apply to a stock company, contact them appropriately (usually listed on their website). Write a short cover letter stating you’d like to send in some images. If you hear back from the company, DO NOT SEND YOUR ONLY ORIGINAL PHOTOGRAPHS!! Make sure you send duplicates of your photographs, so that your work is protected. If you send in photographs by mail make sure you include an envelope with a stamp and return address.
  • Sales on your photographs can take years, so don’t expect money right away.

These companies can charge many fees (that might be negotiable). Note that some of these companies require that they own exclusive rights to market your photograph (you cannot also market the same photograph). Signing up with a stock agency may also limit your ability to work with other stock agency companies. So, before you sign anything make sure the conditions will work with you and your needs. Make sure you understand the contract before you sign anything, such as when your contract is renewed and how to renew or terminate it.
Summary: Just read the whole thing.

Microstock: These companies are usually willing to work with amateur photographers. They don’t charge their clients very much, therefore you will not receive very much money each time a photograph is sold. Microstock images are all royalty free.
Examples of microstock companies:

  • www.bigstockphoto.com
  • www.shutterstock.com
  • www.dreamstime.com
  • www.istockphoto.com

Summary: Similar to stock photo agencies but they’re more relaxed in many ways, and they work with amateurs. You will not make much money with Microstock (unless you’re a photography wizard).

Advertising, Design & Related Markets: This kind of work is always commercial and your photography is used to sell services or products. In order to do this, you need to make appointments to show your portfolio to art directors. You need to show your buyers that you’re easy to work with, and you’re an amazing photographer. The book didn’t seem to go into detail about how to apply, but I would do a lot of research because each of the topics in this category are very vast.

Galleries: Galleries are not a quick source of income and are more a way of creating a presence as a photographer (or artist). In order to be in a gallery, you need extreme quality work. Galleries typically receive 40-50 percent of the commission. You may also need to be present in the show. Make sure you know the kind of gallery you’re applying to and how the different kinds of galleries work. In general, reflect on the kind of work you do and where that work would best be displayed. Statements you write for you work should be short and sweet. The book did not seem to go over how to apply, but I would guess it ranges/changes from gallery to gallery, and you would probably want to write a query/cover letter and have images ready to give as examples.

Art Fairs: Art fairs are a good way to market to an audience that’s looking to buy expensive pieces of work. You’ll need to have framed and unframed work. You need to do research first. After you find a fair that’s a good fit for you, then visit their website and contact the appropriate person to get more details. You’ll equipment to protect your work (a cover for your area, plastic protective sleeves for your photographs, etc.). In order to apply for an art fair, you might have to go through a selective process, and some fairs will only allow one display per medium (meaning only one photograph display, one painting display, etc.). Art fairs almost always charge fees for you to display your work.
Summary: Research the fair, have plenty of framed and unframed work, be prepared for fees, and know that most fairs have a selection process, so your work needs to really stand out.

Contests: Contests are a good way to discover how your work compares with the work of other photographers. Make sure you pay attention to the rights when you submit your images into a contest. You don’t want to accidentally give away copy rights. You may want to view previous images that have won to scope out what style or image typically wins. Contest very vastly, so do your research.

Chapter 3 Terms:
Flâneur

Flâneur is a word used to describe someone who is detached from society and only wonders about observing the world and never being apart of it. This term dates back as early as the 16th century, but the meaning didn’t take shape until the 19th century. Baudelaire made the term famous by putting it into his essay “The Painter of Modern Life” in the year 1863.

Sontag references this term to explain how photographers interact/perceive the world. Like the Flâneur we find joy in watching the world go by. We see what passes, but never truly experience the world in the same way. Sontag shares how the Flâneur is often attracted to the dark and mysterious places of the world instead of the warm and inviting places everyone seems to know. We seek to observe the unordinary, but we don’t necessarily crave to be apart of it.

Chapter 2 Terms:

Alfred Stieglitz "Camera Work" Alfred Stieglitz "Camera Work" "Camera Work," was a magazine published quarterly by Alfred Stieglitz. His magazine was known because some of the most prominent and important photographers had their images featured. The magazine was being published from the years 1903 to 1917.

Why did Susan Sontag reference "Camera Work," and Alfred Stieglitz? She mentions Stieglitz because he was one of the first photographers who stepped away from the idea that only idealized photographs were beautiful. Stieglitz found beauty in photographs that weren't the typical "Ansel Adams" landscapes or photographs of people etc. Stieglitz featured many of the "abnormal" photographs from photographers that are now famous for their work. Stieglitz would only allow photographs to be published in his magazine if they showed something beautifully artistic and were unique, and he would take photographs from someone regardless of their education (when it came to photography I'm assuming). Some of the photographs featured in "Camera Work," were taken by James Craig Annan, Clarence White, Fredrick Evans, Edward Steichen, Frank Eugene, and Alvin Langdon Coburn

Chapter 1 Terms:
Lecia

Lecia is a camera company that was founded in Germany during the year 1914. The cameras are still around today and are marketed towards photographers who want complete control over their camera (They don’t have any automatic options on their cameras even today). In the book, “On Photography,” Susan Sontag references Lecia to discuss how photography has become an “equalizer” for every and anything you could think of. Page 11 in, “On Photography.”

Diane Arbus

Diane Arbus 1923-1971, was known for her unsettling photos and unusual subjects. Arbus was appreciated by some for her display of empathy in her photographs. However, she was also disliked for the belief that she turned unfortunate people into objects. Diane Arbus was close and personal with her subjects. Sontag referenced Arbus to explain the strange intimacy of photography. Page 12 in, “On Photography.”

Alfred Hitchcock’s
"Rear Window"

The movie “Rear Window,” is about a news photographer stuck inside his apartment after an accident that puts him in a wheelchair. The news photographer begins watching his neighbors to pass the time, but soon realizes that he might be a witness to murder. Sontag references the “Rear Window,” to explain how photography is a way to observe and interact with life though you’re not participating directly. Though he’s stuck inside his apartment he feels like some part of his neighbor's life even though he never interacts with them, and most of them don’t know he exists. Page 12.
